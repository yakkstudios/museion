The Hidden Council
Scroll 014 · Specialist Routing · YAKK Studios · April 2026
Æther is one voice. Behind that voice sits a council of specialist agents, each narrow, each expert, each invisible. The user never has to choose a model. The system chooses for them, transparently.

The Generalist Trap

A single large model trying to be everything at once is usually worse than a council of smaller specialists at their own domains. It is also less auditable. When a generalist gives a wrong answer, there is nothing to inspect — the failure is diffuse, the provenance is vague, and the fix is a retrain.

A specialist council inverts this. When a wrong answer is produced, you know which specialist produced it, which inputs it was given, and which component in the chain has to be corrected. The failure is local. The fix is local.

The council grows append-only. A new specialist is added when the need is durable, not when a demo would be neat. Old specialists are deprecated gracefully — still callable, with a deprecation notice, while the replacement runs in shadow.

"A generalist is a specialist who has not yet been asked a hard enough question."

The Router

Æther does not pick a single specialist per query. It decomposes the request into sub-questions, routes each sub-question to the right specialist, and synthesises the responses into a single answer. The router is itself a small model, but it does not answer any question on its own — its only job is to decide who answers.

Routing decisions are logged. Every response ships with a list of which specialists contributed which claims. A user who wants to see the plumbing can ask, and the plumbing is visible. A user who doesn't care sees one calm voice.

Why This Matters for Integration

A partner capability is, architecturally, just another specialist. StreamLock's reputation lookup is a specialist. StakePoint's staking analyser is a specialist. The Alexandria archive is a specialist. A future custody oracle is a specialist. Each one plugs in behind the router, is registered with a named contract, and becomes addressable from the voice the next day.

The partner never has to build UX. The partner never has to match Æther's tone. The partner ships a service that answers a question well, and Æther decides when to call it.

What the Contract Looks Like

Scoped inputs
A specialist receives only the inputs it needs — wallet address, token mint, time window — and never the user's broader context.
Structured outputs
Responses are typed. A claim, a confidence, a set of citations. Prose is rendered by the synthesiser, not by the specialist.
Rate-limited calls
Each specialist has its own rate limit. A partner is protected from being accidentally DDoSed by a viral question.
Observable failures
A specialist timeout or error becomes a visible absence in the response, not a confidently wrong assertion.

Why the Council Is Hidden

A visible council would be a control panel. A dropdown of models, a tab per specialist, a cognitive tax on the user. That surface is fine for power users and wrong for everyone else.

The hidden council is a deliberate choice to put the complexity on the platform, not on the user. The user sees one voice and one answer. The provenance is available on request. The system is legible when you want it to be and invisible when you don't.
