The Five-Layer Stack
Scroll 011 · Architecture · YAKK Studios · April 2026
Every system Museion ships runs on five layers — voice, persona, council, archive, and rails. Each one is a discrete commitment. Together they describe the whole product.

Why Five

A single chat interface can do a lot of damage. It can speak in an assured voice while routing your request to whichever model answered fastest, keep no memory of what you asked last week, pull from whichever corner of the internet is cheapest to index, and execute actions through whatever wallet adapter was convenient at the time.

That is the architecture most AI surfaces are quietly running on. It works until it doesn't, and when it fails it fails silently — wrong answers delivered with the same tone as correct ones, wallets drained on the same rail that was meant to protect them.

Museion is the opposite decision. Five layers, each one explicit, each one auditable, each one replaceable without breaking the others. The whole point is that you can point at any part and say that one — that is the part I'm trusting.

Layer One · The Voice / HUD

This is the surface users touch. Voice-first by design, telemetry-rich by default. Every response renders with the underlying evidence attached — the wallet it's referencing, the cluster it's citing, the scroll it's drawing from. Nothing is asserted in prose without a receipt next to it.

The HUD is not a chat window with a pretty skin. It is an operator console that happens to accept natural language. Live price, holder distribution, risk score, recent anomaly flags — all visible without asking. The voice doesn't replace the panel. It narrates it.

Layer Two · The Æther Persona

A single meta-voice sits above every specialist. Æther is calm, evidence-led, non-sycophantic, and consistent across sessions. It is the only persona the user ever talks to. It doesn't pretend to know what it doesn't — it admits uncertainty, cites sources, and routes under the hood.

This matters for partners because it means the integration surface is stable. You are not integrating against twelve personality quirks from twelve underlying models. You are integrating against one persona that has a documented voice, a documented refusal list, and a documented way of handling ambiguity.

"A house with five rooms is not more complicated than a house with one big room. It is more honest about what happens where."

Layer Three · The Hidden Council

Behind Æther sits a rotating council of specialist agents — on-chain forensics, token-economics analysis, market microstructure, historical pattern matching, legal framing, and more. The user never sees them directly. Æther routes the request to whichever specialist owns the question, synthesises the response, and returns a single answer.

The council is append-only. New specialists can be added without retraining the voice. Old specialists can be deprecated without a visible break. This is the layer where partner capabilities plug in cleanly — a StreamLock reputation lookup, a StakePoint staking calculator, a future custody oracle — each one just another specialist behind the same voice.

Layer Four · The Alexandria Archive

The council does not reason from first principles every time. It reasons from an archive. Alexandria is the permanent memory layer — one hundred historical scrolls spanning empire, trap, behavioural, sovereign-rails, and kaize categories, plus every council record, every anomaly flag, every user-scoped observation that has ever been committed.

Each entry is human-readable, machine-parsable, and linkable. Anomalies are matched against historical precedent before they are communicated. A rug pattern is not called a rug pattern because an LLM felt confident — it is called a rug pattern because it matches the signature of a scroll already in the archive, and the scroll is cited in the response.

Layer Five · The Governed Execution Rails

Nothing Æther says moves money. The execution layer is separated from the reasoning layer by design. When an action is proposed — a stake, a swap, a signature — it is rendered as a bounded transaction with its own confirmation surface, its own rate limit, its own refusal list, and its own audit trail.

The voice can suggest. The rails decide whether the action is allowed, and under what constraints. This is the layer where the non-greedy covenant lives — hard-coded refusals that no amount of prompt engineering or social pressure can route around.

How The Layers Fail Safely

A specialist goes down
The voice returns a partial answer with the missing surface flagged. It does not fabricate coverage.
A model swaps underneath
The persona stays stable. The user never notices the router picked a different backend.
The archive is cold
The response narrows its claims and cites the gap explicitly. No precedent, no assertion of precedent.
The rails refuse
The refusal is the feature, not a bug. The user gets a structured explanation of which guardrail triggered and why.

The Integration Surface

For a partner, this architecture answers the most common procurement question — where do we plug in? — before it is asked. There are four obvious entry points. Each is a different commitment with a different review burden.

Most partnerships start at L3 — a named specialist behind Æther — because that is the shortest path from contract to visible value for shared users. L1 and L5 are heavier integrations reserved for deep custody or product-surface collaborations.

Partner Hook
The Five-Layer Stack is the map. StreamLock and StakePoint are not being asked to integrate with Museion as a whole — they are being offered a choice of layer. A reputation signal behind the voice (L3). A staking panel inside the HUD (L1). A shared archive channel for rug alerts (L4). A permissioned execution lane for joint flows (L5). Each one is scoped. None require rewiring the others.
